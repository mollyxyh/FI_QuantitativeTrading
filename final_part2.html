def realized_gains(weight, CV, S):
    mix = CV*weight + S*(1-weight)
    trading = [0,0]
    for i in range(2, len(sampleB)-5):
        c = mix[i]
        ar = ols(y=mix[:i], x=mix[:i].shift(1))
        for _ in range(5):
            c = c*ar.beta[0]+ar.beta[1]
        if c-mix[i]>0.00001:
            trading.append(1)
        else:
            trading.append(0)

    realize = 0
    for i in range(len(trading)):
        realize += trading[i]*(mix[i+5]-mix[i])
    return realize

Conclusion¶

To conclude, the result in test sample is not as good as that in cross-validation sample.

I think one of the most important reason is that the weight of cointegrated vector used in both cross-validation sample and test sample comes from training sample, which is probably outdated and not suitbale for current market. In this case, the so-called cointegrated vectors built in test sample are not actually cointegrated at all, and therefore the AR(1) models built on them are not robust, which may undermine the corresponding trading profit.

What's more, AR(1) may not be adequate to model the cointegrated vector since it has conditional variances, which calls for ARCH/GARCH.

From the result above for three cointegrated vectors, the first cointegrated vector got the best result and the performance of the second/third two vectors are not good. This means 2Y & 3Y have better cointegrated relationships and have more trading opportunities.

The winning percentage could be raised if we increase the trading threshold (currently 0.1 basic point).

The correlation between forecast and realized gains is far from satisfactory, which may be increased by improvement of model (AR(1) to ARIMA/GARCH) and frequate parameter updates.
